Processor Architectures at RetroFest 2026

Here are the Processor Architectures you can see in our little UNIX corner of RetroFest 2026.

MIPS

The MIPS architecture was first seen in 1985 in the R2000 processor from MIPS Computer Systems. This was followed by the R6000 in 1989, which implemented the second-generation "MIPS II" architecture.

Silicon Graphics were MIPS Computer Systems primary customer, and so they purchased MIPS Computer Systems outright in 1992, renaming them MIPS Technologies. They went on to release the R4000, R8000 and R10K processors used in their own Silicon Graphics machines, the R4300 processor used in the Nintendo N64, and the R5900 processor used in the Sony Playstation 2.

MIPS Technologies was spun back out in 1998, when the architecture was dropped by Silicon Graphics in favour of Intel's ill-fated Itanium. Despite various owners (including Imagination Technologies for a while) MIPS Technologies still exists today.

PA-RISC

Hewlett Packard Precision Architecture (also known as PA-RISC, or HPPA) was first introduced by HP in the TS-1 processor of 1986. The processor launched in the HP 9000 840 Indigo server (no relation to the Silicon Graphics desktop of the same name). That machine cost $113,500 in 1986 dollars. The TS-1 processor was not a silicon chip, but instead spanned several 8" x 11" PCBs featuring 150 individual ICs.

Later PA-RISC processors were integrated onto a single silicon chip, like the PA-7100LC and the PA-7300LC in our HP 9000 Visualize B132L+ desktop.

The final version of the PA-RISC architecture, PA-RISC 2.0, was 64-bit and launched in 1996 in the PA-8000 processor. Our HP 9000 Visualize C3000 tower features a 64-bit PA-RISC 8500 processor.

The architecture was dropped by HP in favour of Intel's ill-fated Itanium.

SPARC

The Scalable Processor Architecture (SPARC) was introduced by Sun in 1987 as SPARC V7 (I have no idea why they started at V7). Processors based on this architecture were used across Sun's lineup (such as the SPARCstation). The SPARC architecture was open-sourced in 1989 and is managed by SPARC International. Apart from Sun (later Oracle), SPARC processors have also been designed by Fujitsu, Texas Instruments and the European Space Agency.

SPARC V8 was released in 1992, in the SuperSPARC series of processors. 1993's SPARC V9 moved the architecture to 64-bit, and was implemented by the UltraSPARC processors (as used in the Sun Ultra line of machines, and OEM boards like our SPARCengine Ultra based tower).

The SPARC processors were phased out by Sun (on the desktop at least) in around 2005 in favour of the 64-bit AMD Opteron. The last SPARC processor from Oracle (who bought Sun) was the server-class SPARC M8 in 2017. The architecture lives on though, especially in spaceflight applications using derivatives of the European Space Agency's LEON processor.

PowerPC

The POWER (or Performance Optimisation with Enhanced RISC) architecture was introduced in around 1990 in the IBM RISC System 6000 (or RS/6000) line of workstations and servers. POWER1 was followed by POWER2 in 1993.

Apple were looking for an alternative to the Motorola 68040 after issues rolling out Macintosh models based on that processor. Out of various options then ended up in a three-way deal with Motorola and IBM - the AIM alliance - to develop the PowerPC Architecture. IBM contributed the technology behind POWER1 and POWER2, Motorola were ready to make large quantities of the desktop-oriented product (something IBM did not want to do), and Apple were ready to buy lots of them.

The first PowerPC chip was the PowerPC 601 - and it was something of a rush job, taking just 12 months from initial design to tape-out. Performance suffered somewhat on Apple machines though, due to Apple continuing to ship vasts amount of Motorola 68K code which simply ran in an emulator. In time, Apple would solve that problem, and they shipped models based on the:

  • PowerPC 603/603e
  • PowerPC 604/604e
  • PowerPC 750 (the 'G3')
  • PowerPC 7400 (the 'G4')
  • PowerPC 970 (the 'G5')

The PowerPC architecture was also used in the Nintendo Gamecube and Wii, the Microsoft Xbox 360, and the Sony Playstation 3. The architecture was renamed to Power ISA and is still used today in high-end IBM systems. PowerPC also has a niche in embedded systems for automotive and aerospace - various rovers on the surface of Mars have been powered by a radiation-hardened PowerPC chip.
